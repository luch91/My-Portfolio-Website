01 / Problem Definition

The Core Economic Failure in AI Products

SaaS pricing rests on a durable assumption: marginal cost trends toward zero at scale. Infrastructure scales sub-linearly with users. Gross margins improve with growth. AI products invalidate each of these. Every inference call carries a real, variable cost. That cost compounds through orchestration layers, retry logic, context inflation, and concurrency pressure in ways that do not diminish with scale and can worsen with adoption.

The more precise problem is variance, not aggregate cost. A team can manage a predictable cost even when it is high. What breaks pricing models is the gap between median user behavior and tail behavior. Power users submitting complex, iterative, long-context queries pay the same price as light users. As those power users grow as a share of the base, blended margins deteriorate without a visible trigger.

The Structural Insight

P90 models tail risk rather than average cost. Traditional analytics tools show you averages. P90 models what happens at the 90th percentile of usage, which is where pricing models break.

SaaS Assumptions vs. AI Product Reality

The Three Structural Gaps

Measurement gap. Standard monitoring tracks average cost per request. Averages hide the variance that damages margins. A team seeing a stable average may have a deteriorating p90 and no mechanism to detect it.

Architecture gap. Pricing decisions are made after architecture decisions. By the time a team models unit economics, the system is in production with established behaviors. Cost architecture and pricing architecture are treated as separate concerns when they are the same concern.

Simulation gap. No standard tooling exists for stress-testing AI product economics. Teams have no way to project what happens to margins under adversarial conditions: a retry cascade, a concurrency spike, a shift in user behavior toward expensive patterns.

02 / Product Vision and Positioning

The Economics Operating Layer

P90 is an economics operating layer for AI products. It is not a general analytics platform, a billing tool, or a revenue optimization system. Its specific function: make the variance problem visible, simulatable, and actionable before it becomes a margin event.

The platform operates on a principle drawn from the source framework: pricing is system design, not a commercial decision layered on top of a technical product. The cost architecture mapper makes system design legible as economics. The stress tester makes architecture decisions testable as financial risk.

Positioning Statement

P90 is the only platform purpose-built for AI product unit economics. Where traditional analytics tools show you averages, P90 models tails. Where dashboards wait for humans to notice, the agent acts autonomously. Where spreadsheets go stale, the platform updates as behavior evolves.

03 / Feature Modules

Seven Modules, Three Structural Gaps

Each module addresses one of the three structural gaps identified in the problem definition. Modules 1 through 3 address the measurement gap. Module 4 addresses the simulation gap. Module 5 addresses the architecture gap. Modules 6 and 7 are platform-wide intelligence and output layers.

MODULE 01
Cost Architecture Mapper
Builds an honest COGS model across all seven cost layers. Retrieval overhead, orchestration calls, retry cascades, and concurrency provisioning routinely account for 30 to 60 percent of true cost but are absent from most financial models. Surfaces hidden cost layers with industry benchmarks.
MODULE 02
Behavior Simulation Engine
Models how different user archetypes hit the cost structure. Five pre-built archetypes: Casual Explorer, Power User, Enterprise Workflow User, Batch Processor, and Edge Case Stress Tester. Outputs tail-risk thinking across p50, p90, p95, and p99 cost distributions.
MODULE 03
Pricing Model Selector
Replaces intuition-based pricing model selection with a structured decision process matched to system behavior. Interactive decision tree across five forks. Outputs a ranked recommendation with confidence score and maturity roadmap showing evolution conditions.
MODULE 04
Margin Stress Tester
Runs adversarial scenarios against the pricing model before users do. Six canonical scenarios: Retry Cascade, Heavy User Cluster, Context Bloat, Peak Load, Model Routing Failure, and Enterprise Onboarding Spike. Outputs margin survival reports and break-even floor.
MODULE 05
Pricing-Architecture Alignment Checker
Flags economic mismatches between pricing model and system design before they compound. Feature economic tagging, roadmap impact previewer, and architecture decision log. Prevents the most common form of institutional amnesia in AI product organizations.
MODULE 06
Living P&L Dashboard
Real-time unit economics visibility: revenue per user, COGS per user across all seven layers, gross margin per user, contribution margin by segment. Behavioral drift alerts trigger when average context length, retry rate, or power user concentration crosses threshold.
MODULE 07
Pricing Playbook Generator
Converts platform outputs into a living pricing strategy document. Auto-generates pricing rationale, tier design recommendations, and pricing review triggers. Includes stakeholder presentation builder with board-ready summaries: model rationale, unit economics snapshot, stress test results, and forward-looking scenarios. Exports as PDF or PPTX.

04 / The Agentic Core

Four-Node LangGraph Agent with Human Approval Gate

The agentic core is a LangGraph StateGraph with four nodes executing in a conditional sequence. It runs on a configurable interval as a background asyncio task. No human input triggers it. The agent monitors, detects, simulates, and reasons autonomously, surfacing output only when the results warrant a decision.

MONITOR
Rule-based threshold evaluation
Reads the last 5 MetricSnapshots from the database. Evaluates six signals against configured thresholds. Outputs a triggered flag with a list of crossed thresholds, or a healthy status that terminates the run.
DETECT
Rule-based layer attribution
Takes triggered metrics and the latest snapshot. Produces an AnomalyEvent record with anomaly type, affected cost layer, and severity classification. Attribution is deterministic.
SIMULATE
Deterministic calculation
Takes the AnomalyEvent and current metrics. Outputs a 30-day margin projection and a cost increase estimate. Creates a SimulationResult record. No LLM in this node: simulation results must be reproducible and auditable.
REASON
LLM: Groq gpt-oss-20b
Takes the AnomalyEvent, SimulationResult, and seven article principles as system context. Produces three ranked PricingOptions with risk levels, article principle citations, and margin impact estimates. Output validated on parse; failure triggers one retry with the fallback model before logging an error state.
The Human Approval Gate

The agent terminates at the Reason node with a pending recommendation. The frontend surfaces an approval panel with three sections: anomaly summary with affected layer and severity, simulation output with projected 30-day margin impact, and three pricing options each labeled by risk level with article principle citation. The decision is logged with timestamp, selected option, approver identity, and the full LLM reasoning chain.

Why Automated Execution Is Not V1 Scope

Pricing decisions affect customer trust, contractual commitments, and competitive positioning. Automating them without an approval gate would be architecturally unsound regardless of model quality. V1 establishes the monitoring, reasoning, and logging infrastructure that makes V2 auto-execution safe: a team can observe agent recommendations for several months, measure approval rate and recommendation quality, and then selectively enable auto-execution on low-risk pricing levers with appropriate guardrails.

07 / Key Product Insights

What This Product Taught Me

Pricing is system design, not a commercial decision. Every architecture choice is a pricing decision made before the pricing conversation. P90 makes that connection visible.
Average cost is a dangerous metric for AI products. The tail is where pricing models fail. Building for median behavior when power users drive margin compression is structurally incorrect.
The human approval gate is not a limitation of V1. It is the correct architectural decision. An agent that can change pricing autonomously without accountability infrastructure creates more risk than it removes.
The seven-layer cost model forces honesty. Eval and guardrails are absent from over 70 percent of self-reported cost models but represent 8 to 15 percent of true COGS in production systems. The model surfaces what teams prefer not to see.
