01 / The Problem

When Multi-Agent Systems Fail, Nobody Knows Why

Every week, organisations around the world are deploying AI systems designed to make complex decisions: approve a loan, schedule a clinical procedure, route a delivery, respond to a cybersecurity incident, process an insurance claim. These AI systems are not single tools. They are multi-agent systems: networks of AI sub-agents that each handle a piece of the decision and pass information to one another.

When these systems make mistakes, nobody knows why. An AI system approves a loan application that should have been declined. A clinical scheduling agent books a surgeon into two operating theatres simultaneously. A logistics agent routes a shipment through a port it does not have clearance to enter. In every case, the organisation gets a wrong answer. What they do not get is an explanation.

Truvyx in One Sentence

Truvyx is the platform that tells you, before and after you deploy a multi-agent AI system, whether it is reasoning correctly, and when it is not, exactly where the failure came from and how to fix it.

Before Truvyx, organisations had three options. Run the system and hope for the best. Hire a team of specialists to manually design test scenarios and write evaluation scripts by hand, at a cost of 4 to 6 hours per scenario. Or avoid deploying the system at all until someone figured out how to test it properly. None of these options were good.

02 / What Truvyx Does

Evaluation Infrastructure That Runs Continuously

Truvyx is built for two types of people in every organisation. The first is the decision-maker: the Chief Risk Officer who needs to know whether the AI system their team is about to deploy will create regulatory exposure. The Chief Operating Officer who needs to know whether the AI system will actually handle the operational complexity of their environment. The procurement lead who needs an objective basis for choosing between competing AI vendors.

The second is the implementer: the engineering team responsible for deploying and maintaining the AI system, who needs evaluation infrastructure that runs continuously, catches problems before they reach customers, and tells them specifically what to fix when something breaks.

The Key Insight for Managers

You do not need to understand how any of these modules work technically. What matters is what they produce: verified test scenarios built from your real operational rules, automatic scoring on every deployment, and plain-language failure reports that tell your team exactly what to fix. Truvyx translates AI system behaviour into language that operations, risk, and compliance teams can act on.

03 / Six Modules

Every Stage of Evaluation, End to End

Truvyx is built around six modules that cover every stage of AI system evaluation: from designing the tests, through running them, to diagnosing failures and improving the system. You do not need to use all six from day one.

01
Scenario Studio
The Test Designer
This is where test scenarios are created. A structured form guides you through describing your operational environment: what resources you have, what rules govern your decisions, what constraints apply, and how many AI agents are involved. The Studio turns those inputs into fully specified test problems: complete operational scenarios that your AI system is asked to solve. An expert can produce 50 high-quality test scenarios in a day, compared to 2 or 3 using manual methods.
02
Constraint Engine
The Rules Validator
Every operational environment is governed by rules: regulations, resource limits, deadlines, approval sequences, compliance requirements. The Constraint Engine is where those rules are formally defined. Once defined, the engine automatically checks whether your test scenarios are solvable, whether any of your rules contradict each other, and generates the correct answer that a well-functioning AI system should produce. This correct answer becomes the benchmark against which your AI system is scored.
03
Verifier Factory
The Scoring Engine
Generates scoring scripts automatically from test scenarios and correct answers: one script per scenario. Each script checks three things: did the AI system follow all the rules (feasibility), did it address everything required (completeness), and how close was its answer to the best possible answer (optimality). These scripts run in your deployment pipeline, scoring every model update before it goes live.
04
Decomposition Designer
The Architecture Planner
A visual planning tool that lets you design and document how a multi-agent division of labour should work: which agent handles which part of the problem, what information each agent needs, and how the agents communicate with each other. It also lets you compare what your AI system actually did against what it should have done, revealing whether the system's internal structure matched the intended design.
05
Benchmark Registry
The Test Library
A version-controlled library of all test scenarios, organised by industry, difficulty, and constraint type. Public scenarios are freely available and used by AI researchers worldwide. Your organisation's private scenarios are stored in an encrypted, access-controlled namespace. The Registry also hosts performance leaderboards showing how different AI systems score against the same scenarios, giving you an objective basis for vendor comparison.
06
Agentic RCA Engine
The Diagnostic Layer
RCA stands for Root Cause Analysis. When your AI system fails a test or produces a wrong decision in production, the RCA Engine traces the failure back to its origin. It identifies which agent made the wrong decision, classifies what type of failure it was, and generates a precise counterfactual: the smallest change that would have produced the right answer. It also monitors patterns across many failures to surface systemic weaknesses before they become systemic problems.
04 / Implementation Path

Start Where the Pain Is Highest

The most common mistake organisations make when adopting evaluation infrastructure is trying to implement everything at once. Truvyx is designed to be adopted incrementally. You can start with a single module, demonstrate value to your leadership team, and expand the implementation as your confidence and needs grow.

1
Define Your Operational Constraints
Work with your risk, compliance, and operations teams to document the rules that govern decisions in your target domain. These become the input to the Constraint Engine. This step alone typically surfaces rule conflicts and undocumented constraints that no team was aware of. Budget 2 to 3 workshops with subject matter experts.
2-3 weeks
2
Build and Validate Your Benchmark Suite
Use the Scenario Studio to convert your constraint documentation into test scenarios. The Constraint Engine validates every scenario for feasibility and generates the correct reference answers. At the end of this step, you have a private benchmark suite that reflects your actual operational environment.
2-4 weeks
3
Run Your First Evaluation
Deploy the Verifier Factory scripts against your AI system. You receive structured scores on feasibility, completeness, and optimality, plus a full constraint violation report. This is typically the step that produces the first major decision-relevant finding.
1-2 weeks
4
Integrate Into Your Deployment Pipeline
Use the Eval SDK to embed the scoring and RCA analysis into your deployment pipeline. From this point, every model update is automatically evaluated before going live. Failures trigger an RCA report routed to the appropriate team. This stage converts Truvyx from a one-time evaluation tool into permanent operational infrastructure.
1 week
For Regulated Environments

If your organisation operates in banking, healthcare, insurance, or government, activate compliance mode and the audit trail format before your first evaluation run. Compliance mode produces cryptographically signed, examiner-ready audit records for every evaluation. This costs nothing additional and ensures that every evaluation run produces documentation that satisfies regulatory examination requirements from day one.

05 / Industry Case Study: Healthcare

Kingsbridge Medical Centre: Catching a Patient Safety Failure Before It Harmed Anyone

Kingsbridge Medical Centre is a 620-bed tertiary referral hospital managing over 2,400 clinical procedures per month across 14 speciality departments. They had invested in a multi-agent AI scheduling system designed to automate the allocation of surgical cases, coordinate theatre and ICU resources, and reduce the administrative burden on clinical coordinators.

Before go-live, the clinical governance team required a formal evaluation. They had seen a neighbouring hospital experience three near-miss incidents during the first month of a similar system's deployment, incidents that the vendor had attributed to configuration errors but that the clinical team suspected were architectural failures in the AI system itself.

What the Evaluation Found

Truvyx modelled Kingsbridge's scheduling environment across 39 benchmark scenarios. The Constraint Engine validated all 39 for clinical feasibility; two initial scenarios were found to contain logically impossible constraint combinations, surfacing an internal policy conflict between the ICU capacity management protocol and the post-operative monitoring standard for cardiac procedures.

The vendor's AI system was evaluated against all 39 scenarios. The overall feasibility score was 88.4%. The RCA Engine identified 7 safety-relevant failures concentrated in scenarios involving high-acuity patients with multiple concurrent procedures.

Fault TypeEventsWhat Went WrongHow It Was Fixed
Constraint Blindness 5 The theatre allocation agent selected theatres based on availability and procedure type, without considering the infection-risk isolation requirement. In two of the five cases, this would have placed an infection-risk patient in a standard theatre immediately before an immunocompromised patient's procedure. Theatre allocation agent's input schema extended to include infection_risk_flag and isolation_class from the patient record agent before theatre assignment runs.
Temporal Misalignment 2 The anaesthetist assignment agent scheduled two senior anaesthetists into consecutive high-acuity cases without checking the mandatory rest interval between cases. The orchestrator ran the assignment in parallel with the theatre booking rather than after it. Orchestration sequence redesigned so anaesthetist assignment runs after theatre booking is confirmed, with the confirmed theatre timeline passed as a mandatory input to the rest interval constraint check.
The Finding That Mattered Most

The infection-risk theatre allocation failure would have placed a patient with an active multi-drug-resistant organism into a standard theatre immediately before a post-transplant patient's scheduled procedure: a scenario with a documented risk of serious nosocomial infection. The vendor's own benchmark had not included infection-risk patients in its scheduling scenarios.

06 / Key Product Insights

What This Product Teaches the Market

Vendor benchmarks are not neutral. They are designed to show what the vendor chose to test, which is rarely the hardest 20 percent of a real operational environment. The only benchmark that matters is one built from your own constraints.
A feasibility score of 88.4 percent sounds acceptable until you learn which 11.6 percent failed. In healthcare, those scenarios were the ones involving the most vulnerable patients. Average scores hide the distribution that determines safety.
Constraint conflicts surface during the constraint definition step, before a single test runs. The ICU capacity and post-operative monitoring conflict at Kingsbridge had existed as an undocumented inconsistency in policy for three years. Evaluation forced the conversation.
Incremental adoption is the correct deployment model. Starting with a single module in the domain of highest risk, demonstrating value, and expanding is faster than comprehensive implementation and produces better stakeholder buy-in at each stage.
