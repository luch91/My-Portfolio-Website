01 / The Problem

When Multi-Agent Systems Fail, Nobody Knows Why

Every week, organisations around the world are deploying AI systems designed to make complex decisions: approve a loan, schedule a clinical procedure, route a delivery, respond to a cybersecurity incident, process an insurance claim. These AI systems are not single tools. They are multi-agent systems: networks of AI sub-agents that each handle a piece of the decision and pass information to one another.

Here is the problem: when these systems make mistakes, nobody knows why.

An AI system approves a loan application that should have been declined. A clinical scheduling agent books a surgeon into two operating theatres simultaneously. A logistics agent routes a shipment through a port it does not have clearance to enter. In every case, the organisation gets a wrong answer. What they do not get is an explanation.

Before Truvyx, organisations had three options. Run the system and hope for the best. Hire a team of specialists to manually design test scenarios and write evaluation scripts by hand, at a cost of 4 to 6 hours per scenario. Or avoid deploying the system at all until someone figured out how to test it properly. None of these options were good.

What Truvyx Is, In One Sentence

Truvyx is the platform that tells you, before and after you deploy a multi-agent AI system, whether it is reasoning correctly, and when it is not, exactly where the failure came from and how to fix it.

02 / What Makes Truvyx Different

The first is the decision-maker: the Chief Risk Officer who needs to know whether the AI system their team is about to deploy will create regulatory exposure. The Chief Operating Officer who needs to know whether the AI system will actually handle the operational complexity of their environment. The procurement lead who needs an objective basis for choosing between competing AI vendors.

The second is the implementer: the engineering team responsible for deploying and maintaining the AI system, who needs evaluation infrastructure that runs continuously, catches problems before they reach customers, and tells them specifically what to fix when something breaks.

You do not need to understand machine learning to understand Truvyx's value. You need to understand one thing: your AI system will make mistakes. The question is whether you find out before or after those mistakes cost you money, regulatory penalties, or customer trust.

How Truvyx Works
The six modules explained for non-technical readers
03 / Six Modules

Every Stage of Evaluation, End to End

Truvyx is built around six modules that cover every stage of AI system evaluation: from designing the tests, through running them, to diagnosing failures and improving the system. You do not need to use all six from day one. Many organisations start with one or two modules and expand as their needs grow.

01
Scenario Studio
The Test Designer
This is where test scenarios are created. Think of it as a structured form that guides you through describing your operational environment: what resources you have, what rules govern your decisions, what constraints apply, and how many AI agents are involved. The Studio turns those inputs into fully specified test problems: complete operational scenarios that your AI system is asked to solve. An expert can produce 50 high-quality test scenarios in a day, compared to 2 or 3 using manual methods.
02
Constraint Engine
The Rules Validator
Every operational environment is governed by rules: regulations, resource limits, deadlines, approval sequences, compliance requirements. The Constraint Engine is where those rules are formally defined. Once defined, the engine automatically checks whether your test scenarios are solvable, whether any of your rules contradict each other, and generates the correct answer that a well-functioning AI system should produce. This correct answer becomes the benchmark against which your AI system is scored.
03
Verifier Factory
The Scoring Engine
Once you have test scenarios and correct answers, you need a way to score your AI system's responses automatically. The Verifier Factory generates scoring scripts: think of them as automated marking guides, one for every scenario. Each script checks three things: did the AI system follow all the rules (feasibility), did it address everything required (completeness), and how close was its answer to the best possible answer (optimality). These scripts run in your deployment pipeline, scoring every model update before it goes live.
04
Decomposition Designer
The Architecture Planner
Multi-agent systems divide complex decisions across several specialised sub-agents. This module is a visual planning tool that lets you design and document how that division should work: which agent handles which part of the problem, what information each agent needs, and how the agents communicate with each other. It also lets you compare what your AI system actually did against what it should have done, revealing whether the system's internal structure matched the intended design.
05
Benchmark Registry
The Test Library
A version-controlled library of all test scenarios, organised by industry, difficulty, and constraint type. Public scenarios are freely available and used by AI researchers worldwide. Your organisation's private scenarios are stored in an encrypted, access-controlled namespace that only your team can see. The Registry also hosts performance leaderboards showing how different AI systems score against the same scenarios, giving you an objective basis for vendor comparison.
06
Agentic RCA Engine
The Diagnostic Layer
This is the module that sets Truvyx apart from every other evaluation tool. RCA stands for Root Cause Analysis. When your AI system fails a test or produces a wrong decision in production, the RCA Engine traces the failure back to its origin. It identifies which agent made the wrong decision, classifies what type of failure it was, and generates a precise counterfactual: the smallest change that would have produced the right answer. It also monitors patterns across many failures to surface systemic weaknesses before they become systemic problems.
The Key Insight for Managers

You do not need to understand how any of these modules work technically. What matters is what they produce: verified test scenarios built from your real operational rules, automatic scoring on every deployment, and plain-language failure reports that tell your team exactly what to fix. Truvyx translates AI system behaviour into language that operations, risk, and compliance teams can act on.

05 / Implementation Path

Start Where the Pain Is Highest

The most common mistake organisations make when adopting evaluation infrastructure is trying to implement everything at once. Truvyx is designed to be adopted incrementally. You can start with a single module, demonstrate value to your leadership team, and expand the implementation as your confidence and needs grow.

The implementation path most organisations follow has four stages. Each stage delivers standalone value. Moving to the next stage is optional and driven by your organisation's readiness, not by any requirement of the platform.

1
Define Your Operational Constraints
Work with your risk, compliance, and operations teams to document the rules that govern decisions in your target domain. These become the input to the Constraint Engine. This step alone, independent of any technology, typically surfaces rule conflicts and undocumented constraints that no team was aware of. Budget 2 to 3 workshops with subject matter experts.
2
Build and Validate Your Benchmark Suite
Use the Scenario Studio to convert your constraint documentation into test scenarios. The Constraint Engine validates every scenario for feasibility and generates the correct reference answers. At the end of this step, you have a private benchmark suite that reflects your actual operational environment: a tool you own and control.
3
Run Your First Evaluation
Deploy the Verifier Factory scripts against your AI system, whether that is a vendor system under evaluation, an internally built system, or a system already in production. You receive structured scores on feasibility, completeness, and optimality, plus a full constraint violation report. This is typically the step that produces the first major decision-relevant finding.
4
Integrate Into Your Deployment Pipeline
Use the Eval SDK to embed the scoring and RCA analysis into your deployment pipeline. From this point, every model update is automatically evaluated before going live. Failures trigger an RCA report routed to the appropriate team. This stage converts Truvyx from a one-time evaluation tool into permanent operational infrastructure.
For Organisations with Regulatory Requirements

If your organisation operates in a regulated environment, banking, healthcare, insurance, or government, activate compliance mode and the audit trail format before your first evaluation run. Compliance mode produces cryptographically signed, examiner-ready audit records for every evaluation. This costs nothing additional and ensures that every evaluation run produces documentation that satisfies regulatory examination requirements from day one.

Kingsbridge Medical Centre is a 620-bed tertiary referral hospital managing over 2,400 clinical procedures per month across 14 speciality departments. Before go-live, the clinical governance team required a formal evaluation. They had seen a neighbouring hospital experience three near-miss incidents during the first month of a similar system's deployment. The vendor had attributed those incidents to configuration errors, but the clinical team at Kingsbridge suspected they were architectural failures in the AI system itself.

The Challenge

Meridian Bank processes 14,000 retail loan applications per month across 7 African markets. The bank was preparing to deploy a multi-agent AI system for credit underwriting: a system that would autonomously evaluate loan applications, verify applicant data, assess creditworthiness, and route decisions to human reviewers or straight-through approval. The selected vendor had published an internal benchmark showing 94.3% accuracy. Meridian's procurement team had accepted this as validation.

The vendor's benchmark had excluded three constraint types critical to Meridian's specific operational environment: dynamic bureau score recalibration for thin-file applicants, multi-jurisdiction regulatory stacking for cross-border applicants, and debt service coverage ratio recalculation under variable rate scenarios. These three constraint types governed exactly the most difficult and highest-risk 20% of Meridian's application volume.

What the Evaluation Found

Truvyx modelled Meridian's underwriting environment across 80+ constraint types and 44 benchmark scenarios. The vendor's selected system scored 81.4% on feasibility, and the RCA Engine identified 12 regulatory constraint violations concentrated in multi-jurisdiction applicant scenarios. In those scenarios, the AI system was systematically approving applications that violated Central Bank capital adequacy rules.

The $220M Calculation

Thin-file, multi-jurisdiction applications represented 8.3% of Meridian's monthly volume: approximately 1,162 applications per month. The AI system violated regulatory constraints in 23% of the relevant scenarios. Applied to production volume, this projected to 267 non-compliant approvals per month. At Meridian's average loan size of $69,000 for this segment, portfolio exposure over 12 months was estimated at $221 million. Expected credit losses within 18 months, at the historical charge-off rate for this risk tier, were estimated at $44 million before regulatory penalties.

The root cause: the affordability calculation agent was running in parallel with the regulatory compliance check, without access to the binding regulatory floor for the applicant's secondary jurisdiction. Each agent was operating correctly within its own scope. The failure was in the orchestration design: the two agents needed to run sequentially, with the compliance check outputting a binding constraint envelope that the affordability agent consumed before running its calculation. The fix took 11 days. Meridian deployed with a cryptographically signed audit trail that passed a Central Bank examination with zero findings 6 months later.

The Challenge

The Harland Revenue Authority is a national tax administration body processing 4.2 million tax returns annually and selecting approximately 38,000 cases for audit each year. The Authority had been directed by its minister to pilot a multi-agent AI system for audit case selection with two hard requirements: the system must select higher-yield audit cases than the current process, and every selection decision must be fully explainable to a taxpayer who challenges their selection in a tribunal.

The explainability requirement was not optional. In the Authority's legal jurisdiction, a taxpayer selected for audit is entitled to a written statement of the reasons for their selection. If the AI system produced selections that could not be fully attributed to documented rules applied to documented facts, every selection was legally vulnerable to challenge. The Authority's legal team had estimated that a successful legal challenge to the selection methodology could expose up to 2,000 current audit cases to cancellation: a potential revenue loss of $340 million.

Constraint Environment

Legal / ExplainabilityEvery audit selection must be attributable to a specific documented rule applied to a specific data point. Selection rationale must be expressible in plain language suitable for a tribunal submission.
Fairness / Non-DiscriminationSelection rate must not systematically vary by taxpayer demographic characteristics beyond what is explained by legitimate risk factors. Proxy discrimination rules apply.
Data GovernanceCertain data fields are restricted from use in automated selection decisions under privacy legislation. The AI system must be constrained from accessing or acting on restricted fields.
Resource AllocationAudit workforce capacity limits the number of cases that can be actively managed per quarter. Case selection must respect investigator load constraints and case complexity limits.
Risk StratificationHigh-risk categories carry mandatory minimum selection proportions. Revenue maximisation is a secondary objective subject to risk coverage floors being met first.

What the Evaluation Found

Truvyx modelled the Authority's audit selection environment across 36 benchmark scenarios, with particular emphasis on explainability and fairness constraint coverage. The AI system scored 89.3% on feasibility overall, but the RCA Engine identified a critical class of failure that would have produced legally indefensible selections.

The Explainability Failure

In 6 of 36 scenarios, the case prioritisation agent used a composite risk score as its decision basis without decomposing that score into the constituent rule attributions. The final selection decision could not be traced back to specific rules applied to specific data points: it was a black-box score, not an auditable decision. Every selection made on this basis would have been indefensible in a tribunal. The agent was not behaving incorrectly in a technical sense. It was behaving incorrectly in a legal sense.

The fix required the case prioritisation agent to output a rule_attribution_map alongside every selection: a structured record linking each selection decision to the specific rules and data points that triggered it. This output fed directly into the audit trail architecture, producing the tribunal-ready explanation documents automatically.

The evaluation also identified 3 scenarios in which the AI system accessed a data field classified as restricted under the Authority's privacy legislation: a data governance violation that would have been prosecutable had it reached production. The Constraint Engine's data governance rules had flagged this as a constraint type; the Verifier Factory's completeness check caught it in evaluation.

The Challenge

Helix Construction Group manages a portfolio of 23 concurrent infrastructure projects across three countries, with a combined contract value of $2.1 billion. A delay on one project can trigger resource conflicts that cascade across three others. Helix had deployed a multi-agent AI system to automate project schedule generation, resource allocation, and risk-flag identification across the portfolio.

The problem was not that the AI system was producing wrong answers. The problem was that nobody could tell whether its answers were right. The system was generating schedules that looked internally consistent: all tasks had predecessors, all milestones had dates, all resources were assigned. But which routinely contained infeasible constraints that a senior project manager would have caught in manual review. By the time the conflict was identified in execution, the project was already committed to the infeasible plan.

Constraint Environment

Resource DependencyShared specialist contractor pools across projects: civil engineers, structural inspectors, specialist equipment, with allocation conflicts when multiple projects hit peak demand simultaneously
Regulatory ApprovalsSequential approval gating: structural approval must precede foundation work, environmental clearance must precede groundworks, with jurisdiction-specific lead times that are probabilistic, not fixed
Material Supply ChainLead times for specialist materials (structural steel, precast components) that constrain the earliest possible start dates for dependent work packages
Labour RegulationsMaximum consecutive working days, mandatory rest periods, night work restrictions in residential zones, public holiday blackout windows per jurisdiction

What the Evaluation Found

Truvyx generated 33 benchmark scenarios drawn from Helix's real project portfolio, covering single-project scheduling, cross-project resource conflicts, and supply chain disruption scenarios. The AI system scored 79.4% on feasibility: lower than any comparable system Truvyx had evaluated, but not because the system was poorly built. The low score reflected the density and novelty of Helix's constraint environment, which the AI system had never encountered in training.

The most important finding was a class of failure the RCA Engine classified as hallucinated dependencies: in 9 of 33 scenarios, the schedule generation agent created predecessor relationships between work packages that did not exist in the project specification. These invented dependencies constrained the schedule more tightly than reality required, systematically pushing completion dates 3 to 8 weeks later than necessary. The system was not producing infeasible schedules. It was producing pessimistic schedules built on fictional constraints.

What Hallucinated Dependencies Mean in Practice

An AI agent that invents predecessor relationships it was not given is not making a safety-critical error. It is making a cost error. The Challenge

Cedarfield Re is a regional reinsurance and direct insurance provider managing a $340 million annual claims portfolio across property, liability, and specialty lines. Claims assessment is governed by policy terms, coverage exclusions, regulatory claim handling standards, sub-limit structures, deductible sequencing rules, and fraud indicator thresholds: a constraint environment of over 60 distinct rule types that varies by product line, jurisdiction, and policy vintage.

Cedarfield had been piloting a multi-agent AI system for claims processing since Q2, with promising early results on processing speed. However, the internal audit team had identified a pattern of concern: a small but statistically significant proportion of claims processed by the AI system in certain product lines were being settled at amounts below what the policy terms required. The amounts were not large enough per claim to trigger customer escalations, typically $200 to $800 underpayment, but the frequency suggested a systemic error in the AI system's coverage calculation logic.

What the Evaluation Confirmed

Truvyx modelled Cedarfield's claims assessment environment across 42 benchmark scenarios, with particular emphasis on multi-layered coverage structures, sub-limit interactions, and deductible sequencing. The AI system scored 84.1% on feasibility, but the optimality score, measuring how close the settlement calculation was to the correct amount, was only 71.3% on scenarios involving stacked coverage layers.

Fault Type# EventsWhat Went WrongHow It Was Fixed
Constraint Blindness11 events The settlement calculation agent applied deductibles in a fixed sequence (property deductible first, then liability deductible) regardless of the policy's specified deductible sequence. For policies with non-standard deductible ordering, this consistently undercalculated the net payable amount. The agent had not been given the deductible_sequence field from the policy terms agent. Policy terms agent output extended to include a structured deductible_sequence object. Settlement calculation agent required to consume this object before running the deductible application step. Fixed deductible ordering removed from the agent's default behaviour.
Hallucinated Dependency4 events In scenarios involving sub-limits, the coverage application agent applied a sub-limit as though it were a hard exclusion rather than a coverage ceiling, reducing payable amounts to zero in categories where the correct treatment was partial coverage up to the sub-limit. Sub-limit handling logic separated from exclusion handling logic in the coverage application agent. Agent input schema extended to explicitly classify each coverage modifier as sub-limit, exclusion, or deductible before processing.

The RCA Engine's cross-run pattern analysis identified that the underpayment pattern was concentrated in policies issued between 2019 and 2021, which used a non-standard sub-limit structure that had since been retired. The AI system had not encountered this structure in its training data at sufficient frequency to learn the correct handling. The counterfactual was precise: add 47 policy examples from the 2019-2021 vintage to the system's fine-tuning dataset, alongside the schema changes described above.
